When combining the concepts of "Hydrological Communities" (number 2) and "Water User Communities" (number 5), "water community" in this context typically refers to groups of people or organizations that are actively involved in the management, conservation, and utilization of water resources. Here are the key aspects:

  • Stakeholders in Water Management
  • Community-Based Water Management (CBWM)
  • Water User Associations (WUAs)
  • Integrated Water Resources Management (IWRM)
  • Water Governance
  • Participatory Approaches
  • Sustainable Practices

In summary, a "water community" in this context focuses on the collaborative efforts of various stakeholders to manage and utilize water resources effectively, ensuring sustainability, equity, and community involvement.

A circular water community is a collective of individuals, groups, organizations, and institutions dedicated to the sustainable management and reuse of water resources through circular economy principles. This community emphasizes minimizing water waste, maximizing water reuse, and recovering valuable resources from wastewater. By integrating decentralized water systems, innovative technologies, and collaborative governance, a circular water community seeks to create a resilient, self-sufficient, and environmentally friendly water management system. This involves active participation from diverse stakeholders, including local residents, businesses, agricultural entities, and government agencies, working together to achieve closed-loop water cycles that support both human and ecological health.

We choose to see a sequence here:

  • First, we decide to decentralize
  • Then, we need a technical solution to make decentralization happen
  • Next, we realize that this technical solution will not work without collaborative governance (in our proposal this is called a coordination mechanism)

Why? Because climate change, supply constraints, and increasing demand are causing increasing water stress which cannot be solved with water from outside the region.

  • Then, we need a technical solution:

Reservoir-/water banking to satisfy demand from agriculture, tourism and urban use using a physical reservoir.

  • Next, we make it work with a coordination mechanism:

Either formalized using contracts between sectors and the reservoir owner, or community-wise, based on trust and self-management. Or something in between.
